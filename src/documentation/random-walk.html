Worksheet - Random Walk

If you have never programmed before, this worksheet is a great way to start. And if you have previously done a little programming you might still find this exercise interesting.

Our objective is to investigate the mathematical idea of a 'random walk'. Imagine that you are standing in the centre of a circle with a radius of 10 paces. But instead of walking straight to the edge, with each pace you make a random turn - up to to 90 degrees left or right. How many paces will it take to reach the edge? You might answer that it is 'Impossible to say, if the turning is truely random!', and that is correct, for a single journey: for example, the picture below shows three such possible random-walk journeys, and each takes a different number of paces:

But can we determine the average number of paces to reach the edge? Yes, if we could perform the experiment many times. But, you might say, 'What's the point?' Well, it turns out that being able to predict the average length of a different kinds of random walk has very significant applications in many different fields of study: physics, biology, ecology, economics ..., even computer science. (You may read more about this on Wikipedia).

So let's get started.
